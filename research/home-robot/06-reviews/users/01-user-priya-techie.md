# User Review — Priya, the Techie

**Persona:** Priya, 34, software engineer at a Bangalore product company. Lives in a 3BHK rental with her husband and a 4-year-old. Owns 2 Echo Dots, a Roomba i3, a Nest Hub Max in the kitchen. Reads The Verge, occasionally tinkers with Raspberry Pi projects on weekends. Earns enough to spend ₹50-100k on a "cool tech thing" once a year.

**Reading:** the project pitch + use cases.

---

## What I think after reading this

OK first off — this is way more than I would build myself. I've got two RPi 4s in a drawer that I keep meaning to do something with, and the closest I've gotten is a Pi-Hole that broke after 6 months and I never restarted it. So if the question is "would I build this," the answer is no, lol.

But let me read it as "would I buy this if it existed" because that's the more interesting question.

## The use cases

**"Where did I leave my keys."** I lose my keys maybe 4 times a month. I usually find them by walking around the house calling them out loud. So... yes? This is a real problem? But honestly I have 3 AirTags now (one on keys, wallet, daughter's bag) and Find My works. So the value is there if I haven't AirTagged the thing yet. **Worth it for non-trackable stuff** — like "where did I put the Amazon return packaging" or "where is the kid's water bottle." Yeah, that's actually useful.

**"What happened while I was out."** Hmm. We have an outdoor camera at the door and our maid has the keys when we're not home. I'd want to know "did the maid come and how long was she here" and "did the courier come" and that's basically it. The current system tells me both of those (door camera). Would I trust this robot to tell me more? Maybe? Like "did the kid get to the snack drawer" — that's something the camera can't see. **This is fine, not killer.**

**"Go check if I left the stove on."** OK *this* one is real. We do this maybe twice a month — leave the house, then on the way to the airport realize we don't remember if the stove is off. Currently we (a) call the maid (b) ask the neighbor (c) panic. A robot that can drive into the kitchen and tell me? **That's actually worth money.** Maybe ₹20-30k of money, not ₹70k.

So one strong use case, two okay ones. That's not bad but it's not "I must have this."

## What I'd actually want it to do

Things I'd add to the spec, in order of how much I'd value them:

1. **Watch the kid for 15 minutes while I cook.** Like, literally — drive to the playroom, keep an eye, alert me if she does something dangerous (climbs on furniture, opens the medicine drawer, etc.). I would pay ₹100k for this if it actually worked. I know you can't promise this and that's fine, but it's the dream.

2. **Tell me the kid is awake from her nap.** She sleeps in the bedroom, sometimes I'm in the kitchen with the exhaust fan and AC on, and I miss her waking up. Robot detecting "she's sitting up, she's calling for me" → notification = real value.

3. **Find my phone when it's on silent.** Weekly problem. Currently I shout "find my phone" at Alexa which works ok but not always.

4. **Reminders that come from the right place.** "Hey, you left the bathroom light on for an hour" said by a robot in the actual hallway is more memorable than a phone notification.

5. **Talk to my daughter and report what she said.** She's 4, she has full conversations now, sometimes I want to know what she's playing without going there. Bit creepy maybe. But also useful.

The find-my-keys / what-happened use cases that you've designed for are not the strongest in my actual life. The strongest is **monitor the kid in another room** which you've explicitly excluded for reasonable reasons (regulatory, reliability, ethical) — but that's a problem because that's the highest-value thing to me.

## Things that worry me

**Will my husband let me put this on the floor of our living room?** Genuinely no. He's tolerated the Roomba but only because it lives in a corner and looks innocuous. A robot with a camera that pans and looks at us = "no, that's creepy, take it back."

**Will it freak out the kid?** 4-year-olds either love this or hate it. Coin flip. If she hates it, the robot lives in the cupboard.

**₹72,000 is a lot.** Roomba i3 was ₹38k and we agonized. ₹72k is like "buy a new TV" money. Robot would have to do something I can demonstrate to my mom for it to feel justified.

**The 3 months of building.** Yeah no, I'm not doing that. I would buy one fully built. Building it is the hobby for someone else. If you mean I have to assemble + flash + configure for 3 evenings, OK maybe. 3 months is "this is my new identity" territory.

**Privacy with the maid.** This is a genuine thing. She comes for 2 hours daily. Is the robot recording her? Does she know? Does she consent? Indian household help dynamics are not the same as a western "everyone in the household has equal stake." We have to think about who is on the camera and who knows.

## Things I really like

- **Auto-docking.** Yes, please. The Roomba does this and I never have to think about it. This is the kind of thing that turns a tech project into a thing I can ignore for a week and it still works.

- **Local-first / event-driven.** I trust this more than "it's always recording and sending to AWS." If it's only recording when something interesting happens, that's a story I can tell my husband.

- **The "ask any device" idea — robot in any room responds.** This is way better than the current Echo where I have to remember which room's Echo I'm closest to.

- **Genuine voice with proper STT.** Alexa misunderstands me 30% of the time, especially in Indian English with kid noise. If this is materially better, that alone is half the value.

- **The robot saying "I can't reach the kitchen because the door is closed."** Yes! That's a real complaint about Alexa — it just fails silently. A robot that *explains* its failures earns trust.

## What would make me actually buy it

Honestly:

1. **Strong child-monitoring story** with proper safeguards. Even if not "watch unsupervised," even just "alert me if she leaves her room" and "tell me when she wakes up" — those are real.

2. **Under ₹50k.** ₹72k is hobbyist territory. ₹50k is "expensive thing for the home." ₹30k is "yeah I'll try it."

3. **Lives somewhere unobtrusive.** If it parks in a corner and only comes out when I call it, fine. If it lives in the middle of the living room, no.

4. **Setup that takes a weekend.** Not three months. I would pay extra for "comes pre-mapped and pre-configured."

5. **Works in my actual house.** I have rugs, I have cables, I have a 4-year-old who throws toys. Whatever the failure modes are, the robot has to handle them or fail gracefully.

## What I think is honest

The robot you've described is not for me. It's for someone with more time, fewer kids, and more interest in tinkering. That's a smaller market than you maybe think, but it's a real market — there are 100s of thousands of "Priyas without kids" in India who'd find this fascinating.

For my version of this — busy professional, kid in the house, want it to just work — you'd need a different product. Same architecture, different positioning, different price.

## Score from me

Would I build this: 0/10
Would I buy this at ₹72k: 2/10
Would I buy this at ₹40k: 5/10
Would I buy this at ₹40k *for the kid-monitoring use case*: 8/10

Make it about kids and seniors and reasonably-priced. That's where the value is for someone like me.
